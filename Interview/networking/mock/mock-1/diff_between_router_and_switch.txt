q) What is the difference between a router and a switch:
========================================================
1) Function:
   - Router: Connects different networks together and routes data packets between
        them based on IP addresses (Layer 3).

   - Switch: Connects devices within the same network and forwards data frames
        based on MAC addresses (Layer 2).

2) Addressing:
  - Router: Uses IP addresses to determine the best path for data packets.
  - Switch: Uses MAC addresses to forward data frames to the correct device.

3) Network Segmentation:
   - Router: Creates separate broadcast domains (different networks).
   - Switch: Creates separate collision domains but operates within the same
        broadcast domain unless VLANs are configured.

4) Routing Capability:
   - Router: Can perform routing functions, including static and dynamic routing
        (e.g., OSPF, BGP).
   - Switch: Primarily performs switching functions; Layer 3 switches can also
        perform routing.

5) Ports:
   - Router: Typically has fewer ports, designed for WAN and LAN connections.
   - Switch: Usually has many ports to connect multiple devices within a LAN.

6) Use Case:
   - Router: Used to connect different networks, such as connecting a home network
        to the internet.
   - Switch: Used to connect multiple devices within the same local network, such
        as computers, printers, and servers.

7) Performance:
   - Router: Generally slower than switches due to the complexity of routing
        decisions.
   - Switch: Faster as it simply forwards data based on MAC addresses.

Example:
========
- A router connects your home network to your ISP, while a switch connects
  multiple devices (like computers and printers) within your home network.
